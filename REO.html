Applicability of Human Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell Models for Drug-Screening in Alzheimer’s Disease

Laurel Heights Secondary School · 2021

This research investigates how human induced pluripotent stem cells (hiPSCs) can be used to create more accurate and ethical models for drug screening in Alzheimer’s disease. Unlike traditional animal or generic cell models, hiPSCs can be derived from patient-specific cells and engineered to express Alzheimer’s-relevant phenotypes, such as amyloid plaque buildup.

The project explored CRISPR-based mutation correction, phenotype acceleration using oxidative stress, and histological staining for plaque detection. These innovations aim to enable faster and more human-relevant preclinical testing of drugs like Colostrinin and anti-amyloid therapies.

Key Factors

Patient-Specific hiPSC Derivation:

  • Reprogrammed somatic cells using OSKM transcription factors
  • Created disease-specific stem cells replicating Alzheimer’s pathology
  • Enabled personalized modeling for drug testing

Mutation Control via CRISPR-Cas9:

  • Used CRISPR-Cas9 for genome editing and mutation prevention
  • Applied both viral and non-viral delivery systems
  • Created nanocomplexes to reduce immunogenicity and improve targeting

Accelerated Phenotype Development:

  • Used oxidative stress to induce Alzheimer-like features faster
  • Reduced model development time from years to days/weeks
  • Enabled early-stage observation of drug impact on plaque buildup

Histological Staining for Analysis:

  • Used Congo Red and Thioflavin S to visualize amyloid plaques
  • Detected changes in plaque concentration before and after treatment
  • Enabled comparison with human and mouse trial results

Relevance to Future Drug Testing:

  • Allows ethical and accurate testing of new drugs like Colostrinin
  • Improves understanding of drug effects on human-specific biology
  • Supports rapid pre-clinical validation of Alzheimer’s therapeutics
