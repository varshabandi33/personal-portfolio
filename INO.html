Neural Modeling for INO Diagnosis

University of Waterloo · 2025

Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia (INO) is a neurological condition often linked to multiple sclerosis, brain trauma, and viral infections, particularly in older adults. It disrupts eye coordination, typically causing difficulty with inward eye movement (adduction) and involuntary eye jerking (nystagmus) in the opposite eye. Although INO can often be treated based on its cause, accurate diagnosis remains a challenge due to the subtlety of symptoms. To support clinical diagnosis, this project developed a computational model to estimate neural input from eye position data and detect patterns indicative of INO. Simulations compared healthy and INO-affected eye movements, highlighting key differences in abduction delays and coordination. The model was validated using real patient data and effectively identified hallmark characteristics of INO. With further testing, including EEG integration and expanded datasets, this tool could strengthen early clinical detection and improve patient outcomes.

  • Re-ran the model using extracted activations to validate output accuracy.

Key Findings

Healthy Eye Movement:

  • Smooth, symmetrical saccades
  • Well-coordinated muscle and tendon responses
  • No signs of glissadic overshoot or instability

INO Movement Signatures:

  • Irregular muscle activation and longer pulse width
  • Uncoordinated neural signals and tendon imbalances
  • Glissadic overshoot and delayed stabilization

Sensitivity to Subtle INO Cases:

  • Model identified INO-like patterns even without obvious delays
  • Highlights neural instability not visible in VDI analysis
