DentalEase - Assistive Dental Hygiene Device

University of Waterloo · 2024

DentalEase is an affordable and user-friendly dental hygiene device designed for individuals with forearm amputations. It minimizes the need for repetitive brushing by automating the toothbrush's motion when the user lifts their arm. The device features a mechanical cradle with components that were modeled in SolidWorks and fabricated using 3D printing and laser cutting. It also includes an Arduino-based EMG signal processing system that filters, amplifies, and detects muscle activity to trigger brushing. The design incorporates lightweight materials, carefully controlled motion, and signal smoothing to ensure that users can brush their teeth with comfort, independence, and confidence.

Mechanism Design:

  • Used a 3:1 gear ratio with a lightweight servomotor to achieve 300 strokes per minute
  • Limited motion range to 0.25 inches for safe and effective brushing
  • Toothbrush cradle adapted to fit standard manual toothbrushes

EMG Signal Processing:

  • EMG signals sourced from trapezius muscle contractions
  • Signal processed through amplifier, band-pass filter, rectifier, and comparator
  • Arduino interprets on/off signals to activate brushing for one minute per contraction

Materials and Fabrication:

  • 3D printed frame using PLA for strength and lightweight support
  • Laser-cut acrylic gears for better precision
  • Plastic bowl and compression sleeve used for comfortable arm support

User-Centered Design:

  • Device attaches comfortably at the elbow and activates with arm movement
  • No need for specialized toothbrushes
  • Designed with accessibility and ease of use in mind

Challenges and Iteration:

  • Improvised fasteners and added cradle support for stability
  • Switched to peak detection to eliminate repeated activations
  • Adjusted circuit values through real-time testing for optimal filtering

Device in Action:

  • Demonstrates the assembled DentalEase prototype in action
  • Toothbrush cradle performs continuous brushing motion automatically
  • Activated by muscle contractions detected from the trapezius using EMG signals
  • Arduino receives the signal and powers the servomotor for one minute
  • Back-and-forth motion replicates the speed of manual brushing (300 strokes/min)
